Executive Snapshot

Steady is planned as a mobile app for parents of toddlers who are already overloaded by tantrums, bedtime battles, hitting, refusals, and public meltdowns. Its wedge is not another parenting curriculum. It is an in-the-moment SOS flow that helps the parent regulate first, then gives one practical script and one thing to avoid.

The strongest idea in the plan is its discipline around the moment of use: 90 seconds, one hand, audio-first, no shame, and no long content library standing between the parent and the next right move. The broader opportunity is to become a daily steadiness ritual: a way for parents to start the day 1% calmer, more grateful, and more ready to meet both the hard and beautiful parts of raising a child.

The Problem It Solves

Parents usually do not fail because they lack advice. They fail because they are dysregulated in the exact moment they need to co-regulate.

Core insight

Existing support is inaccessible

Therapy and parent coaching can help, but cost, scheduling, childcare logistics, and stigma keep many parents out.

Advice arrives at the wrong time

Books, podcasts, and social content work best when the parent is calm. The acute need happens at 5:45pm with a child screaming nearby.

Meditation apps assume quiet

Traditional mindfulness products expect silence and 10 minutes. This use case has one hand, noise, urgency, and maybe 60 to 90 seconds.

Shame makes isolation worse

After yelling or losing patience, parents often believe everyone else is handling it better. That shame suppresses help-seeking.

The gap: a fast, judgment-free tool for the dysregulation moment itself, followed by repair support and carefully constrained peer reassurance.

Project Hypotheses

The plan is organized around testing the riskiest assumptions first.

Pre-validation
H1

Parents will use a phone during or right after a hard moment

This is the riskiest assumption. If parents will not reach for the tool in the moment, the product should pivot toward the calmer aftermath and repair moment.

H2

Regulation-first framing will resonate

The product bets that parents will respond to "regulate yourself first" because it names the real bottleneck without blaming them.

H3

Toddler flashpoints can create natural retention

If the SOS flow works once, the frequent recurrence of hard moments may bring parents back without engagement gimmicks.

H4

A constrained peer loop can reduce shame

The plan favors anonymous "been there" moment sharing before any full forum, limiting moderation risk while testing community value.

H5

Parents priced out of coaching may pay for a smaller tool

The target price is roughly $50 to $80 per year, positioned well below premium parent coaching programs.

H6

The business can work as a side project

The business goal is $3K+ MRR within 18 months, with founder maintenance time at or below 8 hours per week after launch.

Product Shape

The MVP is Loop 1 only. Later loops add depth and retention after the core behavior is proven.

MVP wedge
1

Regulate the parent

A 0 to 60 second guided breath, grounding phrase, haptics, and reframe. It should work with minimal screen attention.

2

Give the next right move

The parent chooses the situation and gets one script plus one thing not to do. Not an article, a card.

3

Close the loop

An optional check-in records the hard moment and can unlock a repair script if the parent lost patience.

Loop 2: between-moment guidance

Short audio lessons, script-first playbooks, and one weekly thing to try. This supports retention without becoming a content swamp.

Loop 3: community

Starts as moderated, no-comment "been there" sharing. Full discussion is intentionally phase-gated behind moderation tooling.

Daily Ritual Layer

A calmer morning loop could make Steady more than an emergency tool: it becomes a small daily practice for choosing the kind of parent you want to be.

New direction
"Today, I do not have to be a perfect parent. I only have to be a present one: steady enough to repair, humble enough to learn, and loving enough to begin again."

Example daily affirmation · Original Steady voice draft for the morning check-in.

What it could feel like

Each morning, the parent receives one grounding affirmation, reflection prompt, or sourced line of encouragement. The tone should feel like a parent being met at the edge of the bed: honest, warm, energizing, and never syrupy.

Why it belongs

The SOS tool helps in the hard moment. The daily ritual helps before the hard moment: it builds identity, gratitude, and emotional readiness one small repetition at a time.

Content formats to test

1

Affirmation

A short original line in the app's voice: "I can be firm without being harsh. I can repair without being ashamed."

2

Reflection prompt

One tiny journal-style question: "What is one ordinary thing your child does that you want to remember?"

3

Sourced quote

A carefully attributed public-domain or licensed quote from a philosopher, psychologist, writer, or adult child thanking a parent.

Product implication: this adds a second loop to validate: daily morning use. It should be measured separately from SOS usage so the team can learn whether Steady is primarily an emergency tool, a daily ritual, or the rare product that can be both.

Onboarding Strategy

Claude's latest major work appears to be the onboarding and account creation redesign.

Plan only

The onboarding plan argues that the first-run experience should not describe the product. It should let the parent rehearse one SOS flow before the next real meltdown. This is a strong fit with the product thesis because the hardest part is not awareness, it is retrieval under stress.

First-run flow

  • Emotional landing screen.
  • Two-tap micro-personalization.
  • Real script card and practice breather.
  • Lock-screen SOS setup prompt.
  • Soft account creation after value.
  • Home screen with the big SOS button.

Account model

  • Anonymous-first Supabase session.
  • Upgrade to claimed account only when useful.
  • Sign in with Apple and email OTP, no passwords.
  • Minimal data collection and explicit account deletion.
  • Notifications delayed until after real SOS value.

Best design decision: "value before identity." For this audience, avoiding a forced signup wall is both a trust move and a conversion move.

Main Risks

These are the things that can break the product even if the planning is good.

Decision risks

H1 may fail

Parents may not open an app mid-tantrum. This must be tested cheaply before committing to a full native build.

Safety and trust risk

The app must never look like a substitute for clinical care. Crisis signposting and clinician review are required before scale.

Content quality risk

The product needs original, concise, humane scripts. If the voice feels generic, copied, or shaming, the product loses its edge.

Side-project scope risk

Community, content, support, legal, and app-platform complexity could exceed the intended 8 founder-hours per week.

Recommended discipline: do not start Phase 1 build until Phase 0 has real evidence that parents understand the wedge, will try it in or near the moment, and show some willingness to pay.

What's Next

Recruit 20 target parents and focus on the last three hard moments: what happened, what they reached for, what they wish they had, and whether they would pay.

Screen the working name, document the not-therapy posture, and identify a licensed family therapist or psychologist to review content.
